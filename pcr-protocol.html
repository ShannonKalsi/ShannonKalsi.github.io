PCR Protocol

Introduction to PCR

The polymerase chain reaction (PCR) is a sensitive and efficient method for amplifying a target DNA sequence into millions of copies. PCR is used in a wide variety of applications, including RT-PCR, sequencing, genotyping, cloning, and more.

End Point PCR vs Real-Time PCR

There are two main types of PCR: End Point PCR and Real-Time PCR (qPCR).

End Point PCR: Measures the PCR product at the end of the cycles, typically visualized using gel electrophoresis. It is often used in applications like RT-PCR, sequencing, and cloning.

Real-Time PCR: Measures PCR product during the exponential phase. The quantity of product correlates directly to the initial amount of template. This type is widely used for quantifying gene expression, assay validation, and various other applications.

Real-Time PCR (qPCR)

Real-Time PCR is a more accurate and precise technique as it collects data during the exponential phase of amplification. The cycle threshold (CT) value is used to quantify the amount of target DNA.

Real-Time PCR can be performed using two types of chemistries: SYBR Green and TaqMan.

SYBR Green vs TaqMan Chemistry

There are two types of chemistry for real-time PCR:

  • SYBR Green: A DNA-binding dye that fluoresces upon binding to double-stranded DNA. It requires the manual design and validation of primers and may require melt curve analysis to confirm specificity.
  • TaqMan: Uses a fluorogenic probe specific to the target gene, providing higher specificity. TaqMan assays are pre-designed and validated, but they are more expensive than SYBR Green.

Quantification Methods

Relative Standard Curve (SYBR method): This method involves creating a standard curve using known quantities of the target to determine the relative expression in unknown samples.

Comparative Ct (ΔΔCt) Method (TaqMan method): This method compares the CT values of a target gene with an endogenous control (housekeeping gene) to determine relative gene expression between different samples.

Components of a Real-Time PCR Experiment

  • Sample: The gene of interest with an unknown quantity.
  • Reference Sample: Used as the basis for comparison (e.g., an untreated control).
  • Standard: A sample with known quantities of the target gene used to create a standard curve.
  • Endogenous Control: A gene that is expected to be expressed at constant levels across all samples (e.g., housekeeping gene).
  • Replicates: Identical reactions containing identical samples and components. Triplicates are typically used for reliable results.
  • Negative Controls: Samples that contain no template DNA to check for contamination.

PCR Experimental Steps

Follow these steps for PCR:

  1. Prepare the PCR reaction mix by combining the following components in a PCR tube:
    • 1x PCR buffer
    • 1.5 mM MgCl2
    • 200 μM dNTPs
    • 0.4 μM of each primer
    • 1 U Taq polymerase
    • Template DNA (50-100 ng)
  2. Gently mix the reaction and briefly centrifuge to collect the liquid at the bottom of the tube.
  3. Program the thermocycler with the following conditions:
    • Initial denaturation: 94°C for 5 minutes
    • Denaturation: 94°C for 30 seconds
    • Annealing: 50-60°C (depending on the primers) for 30 seconds
    • Extension: 72°C for 1 minute per kb of template
    • Final extension: 72°C for 5 minutes
  4. Run the reaction for 30-40 cycles, followed by a final extension step.
  5. Analyze the PCR products by electrophoresis on a 1-2% agarose gel with an appropriate DNA ladder.
