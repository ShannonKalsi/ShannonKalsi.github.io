Western Blotting Protocol

Introduction

Western Blotting is a widely used technique in molecular biology to detect specific proteins in a sample. It is an essential method for studying protein expression, post-translational modifications, and interactions. Below is a detailed protocol to guide you through the process of performing Western Blotting, from sample preparation to final detection.

Procedure

Step 1: Protein Extraction

Lysis Buffer Composition:

  • 1X PBS: 495ml
  • 0.1% Igepal: 5ml
  • 0.5% Deoxycholic acid: 2.5g
  • 0.1% SDS: 0.5g

Store the lysis buffer at -20°C in 1ml aliquots. Prepare the following:

  • Add 1ml of lysis buffer and 10μl of protease inhibitor (keep on ice).
  • Re-suspend each cell pellet in ~80μl of the mixture. Place on ice for 3 x 10 minutes with 10-second vortexing in between.
  • Spin down at 13,000rpm for 20 minutes at 4°C in Eppendorf tubes.
  • Remove the supernatant (protein fraction) and place it in a fresh Eppendorf tube on ice. Discard the pellet.

Step 2: Protein Quantification using BCA Assay

Make up the standard as below:

Stock (μl) dH2O (μl) Concentration (μg/ml)
0 100 0
10 90 200
20 80 400
30 70 600
40 60 800
50 50 1000
60 40 1200
70 30 1400

Steps for Protein Quantification:

  • Make up standards in 1.5ml Eppendorf tubes and mix well.
  • Make a 1:10 dilution of each protein lysate from step 1 with dH2O (e.g., 6μl sample + 54μl dH2O).
  • Pipette 25μl of each standard and sample in duplicate into wells of a 96-well plate.
  • Prepare the BCA reagent: mix 1 part reagent B (blue) to 49 parts reagent A (clear), mix well, and conserve reagents.
  • Add 200μl of the reagent mixture (A + B) to each well of standards and samples.
  • Incubate at 37°C for 30 minutes (solution will turn purple if protein is present).
  • Cool for 2 minutes at RT and read absorbance at 560nm.
  • Calculate the protein concentration using the standard curve obtained in Excel.

Step 3: Preparation of Samples

Create your samples for 20-50ug of protein

  • Pipette the calculated amount of sample into the Eppendorf tube.
  • Add 2X or 6X Laemmli buffer and mix well.
  • Boil the samples at 95°C in a heating block.
  • Flick spin samples in a tabletop centrifuge.

Step 4: Protein Electrophoresis

Running and Transfer Buffers:

  • Running Buffer (10X) pH 8.3:
    • Glycine: 144.13g
    • Tris Base: 30.28g
    • SDS: 10g
    • dH2O: 1L
  • Transfer Buffer for Semi-Dry Rig (10X) pH 7.5:
    • Glycine: 29g
    • Tris base: 58g
    • SDS: 3.7g
    • dH2O: 1L

Steps for Gel Preparation and Running:

  • Clean the glass plates thoroughly with 70% IMS and assemble them with gaskets.
  • Prepare the resolving gel solution and pour into the gel plates up to the mark.
  • Cover the running gel with isopropanol during polymerization to ensure leveling.
  • Prepare the stacking gel and pour it once the resolving gel has set.
  • Fill the gel system with running buffer and load samples.
  • Run at 110V until the dye front reaches the bottom of the gel.

Step 5: Transfer of Protein from Gel to Nitrocellulose

Semi-Dry Transfer:

  • Prepare nitrocellulose sheets and filter paper by soaking them in transfer buffer.
  • After the gel run, place it in transfer buffer and mark a corner of the gel.
  • Place filter paper and the gel in the semi-dry apparatus and ensure no air bubbles are present.
  • Run the transfer at 250 mA for 1 hour and stain with Ponceau S.
  • Block the blot with 5% milk protein TBST for 1 hour at room temperature.
  • Incubate with primary antibody overnight at 4°C.
  • Wash 3 times with TBST for 10 minutes and incubate with secondary antibody.
  • Prepare ECL and wash 3 times with TBST for 10 minutes.

Wet Transfer:

  • Prepare as for semi-dry, but ensure the nitrocellulose sheet is placed opposite the gel.
  • Run the wet transfer at 60V for 1.5 hours.
  • Follow the same subsequent steps as semi-dry transfer.

Tips and Notes

Properly boil the samples in Laemmli buffer to denature proteins and ensure they separate correctly during electrophoresis.

Do not overload the gel with too much sample, as it may cause smearing and poor resolution.

Always check the molecular weight of the proteins of interest to choose the appropriate gel concentration.
